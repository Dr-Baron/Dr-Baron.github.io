Travel notes in India

Madras - Kanchipuram - Mahabalipuram - Bombay - Jaipur - Amber - Fatehpur - Sikandra - Agra Delhi

December 2002 - January 2003

 

Part II. "They led an elephant through the streets" (from the life of the Maharajas)

 

In ancient times, India (including Pakistan and Bangladesh) was beautifully divided between 435 maharajas, who ruled as best they could, built palaces, accumulated wealth, had fun, often fought with each other, using the support of their maharaja friends and other mafia, and then made peace. The latter was the easiest; it was enough to marry the daughter of a neighboring maharaja. Therefore, many of them had dozens of wives, but "visited" only two or three, and the remaining wives could eventually have only a political purpose.

 

Maharajas exist today. With India's loss of independence, and then its acquisition, they lost their former power, but retained the property, furniture, and jewelry of the dynasty. Once powerful and influential maharajas lived in Rajasthan, a large state in northwestern India. This chapter is about Rajasthan, a state of deserts, colorful attire, and snake charmers.

 

 

Airports, planes and Mumbai (Bombay)

 

Bombay has a distant relation to Rajasthan. It was just that because of the total fog in Delhi, I couldn't get a connecting flight and was sent to Jaipur via Bombay.

 

The difference between the two worlds on either side of the gates of Chennai airport was striking. The airport was a normal civilisation: computers, flat screen TVs showing cricket and English football, an internet centre. Excellent service, everyone smiling, and in a kind way. Fog in the north had disrupted the schedule of half the flights in the south, but a specially appointed worker was constantly hovering in the waiting room and very competently answering questions from passengers stuck due to the weather. At this time, his employee Charu was reissuing tickets for all passengers at once, simultaneously talking to them and to someone else on the phone, getting luggage from planes that had not taken off and literally putting passengers on new flights by the hand. A dozen Americans would have probably been stuck in her place.

 

Strict control when boarding the plane. All bags are searched, all passengers without exception are patted down, at least twice: at the entrance to the waiting room and at the plane itself on the airfield. Bags are x-rayed at the airport, then they are opened and manually inspected upon boarding.

 

In Bombay, the stranded people were given hot tea for free. That Indian Masala tea, brewed in milk, with spices and herbs. But I only had three hours between planes, and grabbing a glass of tea, I ran to a taxi hoping to ride around and meet Raj Kapoor's heroes. True, it turned out to be too far to the center (for reference, Bombay is a huge city with a population of about 15 million, second only to Calcutta). So we drove around the nearby areas and along the shore of the Arabian Sea. Thus, we "tasted" Bombay.

 

Compared to Madras, in Bombay the rich are even richer and the poor are even poorer. There are decent white-stone hotels, even by American standards, and completely dilapidated but habitable shacks nearby. In addition, the city looks much more civilized. You can see girls in jeans on the streets!!! The streets are cleaner and there are almost no stray cattle. Although... one day an ELEPHANT was walking towards us on the street!!! With tourists on its back.

 

And in other respects, it's the same: the same traffic without rules, and cars passing each other 5 centimeters apart, and the main part of a car is the horn, and the streets are turned into markets (but still not all of them!), and men just as carelessly urinate right on the street. "Bombay, brother," as Mr. 420 once heard.

 

 

Jaipur

 

The capital   of Rajasthan and the first planned city in India. Built in 1727 by order of Jai Singh, the Maharaja of the nearby city of Amber. The capital   of Rajasthan. Of course, the Maharaja himself built the city and moved the capital there to become famous.

 

The city is cleaner than Madras, dirtier than Bombay, and much more beautiful than both. Many beautiful palaces and mosques. Such bizarre, unusual architecture. The center of the city - "Pink City" ( Pink City ), surrounded by a pink wall. By law, all buildings within the wall must be pink. Camels with carts walk around the city, among the cars, and again skinny cows and oxen wander. There are an unusually large number of donkeys on the streets.

 

Pink City: Camel, Rickshaw, Auto Rickshaw, Car and Bus on the Same Street

 

The City Palace is the residence of Jaipur's founder , Maharaja Jai ​​Singh, and all subsequent Maharajas, including the current one. The current Maharaja, Swai Bhavani Singh, lives in the palace, and when he is there, his flag is also flown above the palace, in addition to the flag of Jaipur. Inside the palace, however, not all the buildings are pink - the Maharajas are above the law.

 

                

The City Palace in Jaipur, the residence of the current Maharaja Swaii Bhawani Singh

 

The Palace of Winds ( Hawa Mahal ) . In fact, this is the facade of the City Palace. Maharanis - the wives of the maharajas - could look out onto the street through the windows and bars of the Palace of Winds. This large red building on the main street of old Jaipur serves as an emblem, a calling card of the city. On the sidewalk in front of the Palace of Winds sat two people in turbans and played the flute, and in front of them, two charming live cobras rose from sacks! All four of them took pictures with anyone who wanted them for 10 rupees.

 

Palace of the Winds and two snake charmers on the sidewalk with their cute pets

 

Jantar Mantar Observatory . An amazing astronomical and astrological observatory, created by Maharaja Jai ​​Singh himself in 1730 to study sciences, constellations, horoscopes, etc. Situated in the palace itself. The observatory is filled with a number of buildings of an exclusively scientific nature :

 

-           several sundials of varying accuracy. The larger, the more accurate. The largest, the size of a fortress, are listed in the Guinness Book of Records. They show time with an accuracy of 2 seconds!!! I checked it myself.

 

-           twelve interesting structures corresponding to the twelve signs of the zodiac. All of different heights, and turned towards the patron stars. All are equipped with protractors for more accurate astrological calculations.

 

-           two huge marble maps of the starry sky on concave hemispheres.

 

-           a bath with water and a suspended cast iron protractor. Eclipses were predicted quite accurately by the lunar and solar reflections in the water. A separate pavilion determines the angle between the sun and the horizon by the cast shadow. A rotating suspended cast iron circle with a protractor for studying the constellations in the sky.

 

-           and much more .

 

There are monkeys running all over the observatory! The guide said they were wild, they just came. Later I saw many monkeys on the way to Agra, in Agra and in Delhi. And in Delhi there were also green parrots.

 

Jantar Mantar Observatory

This sundial is not the largest. It shows time with an accuracy of 2 minutes. The semicircle is the dial, the shadow is the hand. The left part shows the time in the morning, the right - in the afternoon . The hypotenuse is tilted at an angle of 27 degrees, which corresponds to the latitude of Jaipur.  

With this tool my horoscope is calculated. Therefore it is oriented towards Mars and the constellation Aries. At the moment when the sought constellation crosses the local meridian, it is necessary to look into the tube, determine the coordinates of the sought planet, and the horoscope is almost ready!

 

 

Miniature drawings . The palace housed a whole workshop of an unusual genre - miniature drawings. These are pictures made in a special way. All the paints are obtained from natural precious stones by rubbing the stone against paper in a drop of water (the artist immediately demonstrated this). The paper is made of rice to better absorb the paints. The brush is made of chipmunk hair, and at any given moment only one hair paints, while the rest are needed to hold the paint. There were 200-year-old drawings on display, not at all yellowed, and nearby, in the gallery, you could buy modern ones, but in my unenlightened opinion, not inferior to them. The artists sell them themselves. In the drawings of the maharajas and maharanas, each hair and each wrinkle was separately drawn ! The artist suggested examining the drawings under a magnifying glass. He vividly sketched a separate maharana in my book:

 

 

 

The weapons room. I especially liked the daggers and dirks with two pistols attached to them on the sides. Cold and firearms at the same time (all-in-one)! And also the scissor daggers: when you hit the enemy, whack, and the scissors open.

 

Nearby stood two silver vessels with water, 900 liters each. Also from the Guinness Book: the largest all-metal vessels in the world. Maharajas were afraid of poisoning; when going on a campaign, they took water with them. Continuing the campaign theme, there were two carriages for the maharani, a summer and a winter one. They were carried by hand. The winter carriage looks more like a nailed-down coffin. And next to it are scrolls with prayers in Sanskrit - also for campaigns. An excellent campaign set.

 

Highway from Jaipur to Amber. Nothing special: elephants, buses...

 

Amber

 

Amber is a city near Jaipur that once had its own maharaja. The fortress and palace on the mountain were built by Maharaja Mansingh II in the 16th century. We went up this mountain on an ELEPHANT!!! A warm, woolen elephant slowly, waddling, dragged us up, loudly blowing through its trunk every 10 steps. The guide and I sat on the elephant's back in a kind of baby cot with a blanket. Boarding and disembarking was done from a special platform, approximately at the level of the 2nd floor. The driver in a turban sat on the elephant's neck. He never kicked his elephant, and they communicated exclusively with commands. Once, a driver passing by barked something to his elephant, and the elephant picked up a piece of paper from the ground with its trunk and gave it to its master.

 

  

 

Unfortunately, the palace utensils and furniture were completely missing. This is the fate of most Indian museums. Everything has long been plundered by both conquerors and local residents. Only the functioning temples and the Taj Mahal have their original appearance. Hindus especially mourn the imperial Peacock Throne, strewn with rare precious stones, taken from Delhi's Red Fort to Persia. It is still in Tehran.

 

The most interesting part of the palace is connected with the maharanis. Mansingh II had 12 wives, for whom 12 separate apartments were built. From the maharaja's chambers there were 12 different paths leading to them. The scheme was thought out in such a way that the maharanis did not know and could not guess which of them was the most beloved wife, and who was given more attention by the maharaja.

 

In both palaces, in Jaipur and Amber, women were not allowed into the front courtyards. Including the maharani. They were only allowed to watch from the wall, through a fine lattice. In these conditions, the maharanis led a rational life. While the maharaja received guests and was engaged in state affairs, the maharanis made friends, played chess, dice, cards, and went for walks on the palace wall. In short, they had bachelorette parties.

 

And the founder of Jaipur, Jai Singh, outdid everyone and had 27 wives. The entire family, including numerous sons, lived in one palace. The eldest son became the maharaja, and the rest were warriors who were supposed to obediently guard the peace of the maharaja.

 

The current Maharaja (Swai Bhawani Singh) has only a daughter, and she could have become Maharana, but she decided to pass on the now meaningless title directly from her father to her son.

 

 

Crafts

 

In various cities I asked to be taken to some of the craft workshops that were most typical for the area. Rajasthan is famous for its carpets and jewelers, Agra produces marble products, and Kanchipuram produces world-famous silks. It is extremely easy to organize such visits. The workshops had good reason to hope to sell me something from their products, and the person who brought me there received a fat commission each time.

 

Carpet factory.

 

Carpets are made by hand. The quality of the carpet is determined by the number of knots per square inch. They manage to squeeze from 144 to 1200 knots into 1 square inch! The knots are tied by a person who memorizes the sequence of colors in each row. This sequence sits in his head like a tongue twister. One master cheerfully "sang" his tongue twister to me. He sang it in his own language, but in translation it was something like "two blue, red, four green, blue, two red" etc. And it was sung to a cheerful melody. When the row is finished, he instantly memorizes the next tongue twister, takes out his hook and ties the knots of the next row. What an experience!

 

 

Jewelry workshop .

 

This workshop was located right in the apartment of the chief master and was a family business. Several people sat cross-legged at a long table in a semi-dark room and sharpened metal over a fire. The rest set precious stones and polished them. The working day, of course, is not standardized, but there are no overseers. They work while they work, because the salary is not by the hour, but for the product.

 

 

On the road to Agra

 

From Rajasthan, driver Sunil took me to the neighboring state of Uttar Pradesh and his hometown of Agra (a small, quiet town, he said, of only 2.5 million people).

 

On the way we met the following divas:

 

-           individual camels and camel caravans. People carried wood for firewood and hay for cattle on camel carts. According to Sunil, camels are much cheaper than horses here. A young, beautiful and healthy camel from a good family costs 20-25 thousand rupees or $400-$500!

 

-           cow dung laid out to dry! This is another (we would say - alternative) and very popular fuel.

 

-           dancing bears. People make money by making a bear stand on two legs and jump for a stick in the hope that passers-by will stop and give the bear a tip (maybe honey?). Sunil called them gypsies. This business is illegal, because the bears are essentially tortured, with a rope threaded through their noses.

 

-           roadside prostitutes who have gathered in a specific village. They look like locals, but are dressed more elegantly and have made-up faces.

 

-           women with vessels or dishes on their heads . Sometimes even two vessels, one on top of the other. Many carry ordinary water home this way.

 

-           homemade cars. Peasants have learned to make their own vehicles, the engine for them is taken from a petrol pump. Of course, they have no headlights or any identification marks, and often such a car carried 10-12 people at once, both inside and on the side and in the back on the steps.

 

 

A caravan of camels appears from the fog and soon disappears into the same fog.
