Travel notes in India

Madras - Kanchipuram - Mahabalipuram - Bombay - Jaipur - Amber - Fatehpur - Sikandra - Agra Delhi

December 2002 - January 2003

 

Part III. Treasures of Agra (from the life of emperors)

 

The rest of the story is about the mighty Mughal dynasty that ruled India in the 16th-19th centuries. Descendants of Genghis Khan and Timur the Lame at the same time, they were destined to conquer something. And so, deprived of his possessions, Babur came to India from Samarkand, called his warriors to jihad, defeated the Lodhs in the key battle near Panipat in 1526 and became the emperor of India. This chapter is about the Mughal Empire and Mughal architecture, magnificent temples, palaces, mausoleums and the incomparable Taj Mahal.

The first (and main) Mughal emperors were named:

  1. Babur
  2. Humayun
  3. Akbar
  4. Cihangir
  5. Shahjahan
  6. Aurangzeb

Power passed from father to son, but not necessarily to the eldest. As a rule, there was a struggle for power between sons - and with the help of persuasion, connections, intrigues, promises of high position, with the participation of wives and other relatives, one of the brothers won and became the next emperor.

After Aurangzeb, there were no strong rulers among the Mughals, but their dynasty lasted until 1857, and then was abolished by the British.

Akbar is considered the greatest of the Mughals, who built Agra Fort, moved the capital to Agra (in 1565) and built the isolated fortress of Fatehpur 44 km from Agra and the Sikri temple nearby.

 

Fatehpur Sikri

Having 500 wives, Akbar failed to have an heir in 16 years of family life. There was no way out, and he came to Fatehpur in search of the holy Sheikh Salim Chishti. The Sheikh's prayers were heard, and soon Akbar had 3 sons.

The tomb of the holy sheikh is in the courtyard of the Sikri temple. People come, tie a red thread to the grate at the tomb, make a donation and ask the saint to send them children. When children appear, they come again, donate even more and untie their thread (um, how do they know which thread is whose?)

Fatehpur - Akbar's palace made of red sandstone: throne room, reception hall, entertainment hall, ... ... In the courtyard - markings of stones for the game pachisi. Two people play, moving figures on the field. However, Akbar replaced the figures with beautiful girls, and they gracefully moved from one field to another.

It is still not fully known why the city was abandoned one day. Much later, after the end of the Mughal rule, furniture, jewelry and cladding were stolen. That is why this place is called the dead city.

Agra

And here we are in Agra, with a stunning view of the Taj Mahal from the opposite bank of the sacred river Jamuna. It's hard to tear your eyes away, really. But the Taj Mahal tour is tomorrow.

Taj Mahal on the banks of the drying up, though sacred, Jamuna River

There is a reason why the Jamuna is a sacred river. The fact is that Hindus do not bury bodies. They cremate bodies and scatter the ashes into the Ganges. The Ganges itself is considered sacred if only because, despite all the ashes that fall into it, its water is clean and clear. They say that any bottled or tap water will leave sediment after a while, but the water of the Ganges will never do so.

But let's say that the sacred Ganges is far away, or the person is too poor to take the ashes of a relative to the Ganges. Then he finds a river flowing into the Ganges and pours his relative's ashes there. For example, into the Jamuna, which makes it sacred too. By the way, Delhi itself is also located on the Jamuna , very convenient for Delhi funeral parlors.

If there is not even a Jamuna at hand, then the ashes are poured into any river and they pray that it... well, somehow gets into the Ganges.

Sikandra, Akbar's mausoleum

A symmetrical complex made of red stone, similar in shape to the Taj Mahal, as well as the burial places of Humayun and Safdarjang in Delhi and the main mosques. The same style - Mughal architecture. Much in this architecture corresponds to the description of paradise in the Koran. It suggests fourfold symmetry, a Persian system of gardens and ponds, Mughal pentagonal arches with smoothed corners, reflecting reservoirs and canals intersecting at right angles, a front entrance with huge luxurious gates in front, two on the sides. The mausoleum itself is not always visible outside the complex - ordinary people were not always allowed to look at such beauty. The exception is the Taj Mahal, which is visible from afar from many points. Arches, domes, minarets are also taken from the Persians. Similar designs can be found in Iran, and in particular, in Tehran. By the way, the Mughals spoke Farsi.

The pinnacle of Mughal architecture is, of course, the Taj Mahal. And its first example is the tomb of Timur the Lame in Samarkand.

  

Sikandra, the tomb of Emperor Akbar

Agra Fort

This is a large fortress where Akbar, Jihangir and Shahjahan lived when Agra was the capital of India. It is surrounded by a moat, and in those days it was teeming with snakes and crocodiles. Suspension bridges. The buildings are again in the Mughal style. However, Emperor Jihangir built a courtyard in the Indian style for his Indian wife, nothing Muslim. His other wives were Muslim, but he loved his Indian wife, and had children only with her (so Shahjahan is three-quarters Hindu, the guide proudly noted).

The furniture and jewelry had long since been stolen. The famous imperial golden Peacock Throne, all in diamonds and gems, was taken to Turkey, from there to Persia, where the Iranian shahs sat on it until the arrival of Ayatollah Khomeini.

It seems to me that some of the treasure stolen from Agra Fort was divided between Jonathan Small and the three convicts in the Andaman Islands.

At the same time, the most famous, the saddest and the most romantic part of the Fort is called Musamman Burj. These are the apartments of Empress Mumtaz Mahal, her mini-palace. One of its towers has a special destiny. From it you can see the white marble Taj Mahal in the distance. Here, in endless melancholy, the deposed Emperor Shahjahan spent his last years.

  

Agra Fort



Taj Mahal Again

The history of Taj Mahal is a sad love story of Emperor Shahjahan and the beautiful Mumtaz.

Shahjahan was born in 1590. He was the second son of Emperor Jihangir, but in the struggle for power he defeated his elder brother.

One day (in 1611) he met a Persian girl, Archjumand Banu Begum, in the royal bazaar and fell in love with her at first sight. With her father's permission, he married the 19-year-old Archjumand, whom Jihangir named Mumtaz Mahal (Light of the Palace, a ray of light in the dark kingdom).

Shahjahan loved his wife so much that over the next 19 years she gave birth to 14 children, of whom, however, only 4 sons and 3 daughters survived. In addition, she had 3 miscarriages, for a total of 17 pregnancies. Despite the pregnancies, Mumtaz accompanied her husband everywhere, even at receptions of statesmen and in battles . She was not only Shahjahan's favorite (albeit third) wife, but also his loyal advisor in important state affairs.

In 1631, Mumtaz died while giving birth to her 14th child. She was 39 years old. She gave birth and died during a military campaign, almost on the battlefield (good husband!). At her deathbed, Shahjahan vowed to build her a mausoleum, the likes of which this world had never seen. It was her last request.

Shahjahan was inconsolable. According to the chronicle, he turned grey overnight as if he had been doing it for two years. Immediately after Mumtaz's death, he lost interest in everything and only supervised the construction of the Taj Mahal (translated as Crown Mahal or Crown Palace). The country declared a 2-year mourning.

Taj Mahal

The Taj Mahal was built over a period of 22 years (1631-1653). From sunrise to sunset, every day of the week except Friday, 20,000 people worked on the construction.

It is believed that on the other bank of the Jamuna, Shahjahan planned to build a second Taj Mahal of black marble, as a reflection, a mausoleum for himself, and connect both Tajs with a silver bridge across the Jamuna. However, my guide Rajiv rejects this hypothesis. According to him, a French traveler saw the construction opposite the Taj and spread a rumor about a possible second Taj Mahal. In fact, it was the Moon Garden that was being built, where Shahjahan came to see the Taj Mahal.

Shahjahan's fate was sad. In 1658, in a struggle for power, his third son Aurangzeb killed his three brothers and put Shahjahan under house arrest in Agra Fort. Shahjahan spent his last years in the Musamman Burj, the chambers of his beloved Mumtaz, gazing at the Taj Mahal from the tower for hours. Shahjahan died in 1666 at the age of 76. He left no will as to where he should be buried. Therefore, Aurangzeb buried him in the Taj Mahal itself, reuniting him with Mumtaz. Shahjahan's tomb is next to Mumtaz's, but is larger and higher than it, since he was once an emperor. This tomb is the only detail that breaks the absolute symmetry of the Taj Mahal.

Aurangzeb was the most ambitious of Shahjahan's sons. To keep him from disturbing the peace in the palace, Shahjahan sent him to the army to fight the most important battles for 20 years. It is not often that the son of an emperor fights alongside ordinary soldiers. Thus, Aurangzeb gained popularity among the troops. In the end, the army helped him to power.

  

  

Taj Mahal

From a distance, the Taj is irresistible. One can easily imagine Shahjahan gazing at the Taj Mahal from the Musamman Burj for days on end.

The Taj Mahal is striking in its precise proportions and symmetry, and how it changes at different times of the day, in sunny and cloudy weather, and also changes when you approach it. From a different distance - a completely new impression. From afar it is like a weightless toy, from behind the front gate or from the mosque (as the imam showed it) - like a solid white marble wall, but as soon as the minarets come into view, it becomes much smaller. As you walk towards the central building of the Taj Mahal, the angles between the arches, domes and minarets change, creating new effects. From different angles - different views.

The minarets are slightly tilted: according to the book - so that in case of an earthquake they would not fall on the Taj, according to Rajiv - so that from a distance they would appear vertical. The minarets only serve as decoration, since the Taj is not a mosque, and no one called to services from these minarets.

However, on the side of the Jamuna River, there are two mosques. One is functioning, and the imam there greeted me quite cordially, showing me various optical effects of the Taj Mahal. This imam is the only person I met in India who not only did not ask for, but also refused to accept money. Every day at 5:00 PM there is a service, and on Fridays the Taj Mahal is open from 9:00 to 12:00, free of charge; at noon it closes and a service is held for a crowd of Muslims. The other mosque is not a real one, it is there solely for symmetry. It cannot serve as a mosque, because it is turned the wrong way.

But the inside of the Taj is not as interesting as the outside. An octagonal hall of white marble, with two tiers of Mughal windows. The burial places of Shahjhan and Mumtaz are below, and they are not allowed in there, and on the surface they made beautiful sarcophagi, surrounded by a marble wall. By the way, it is very similar to the tomb of Humayun in Delhi.

It seems that in all of India, only the Taj enjoys the care of the state. It is renovated every year, and when I was there, workers with brushes and mops were literally hanging on one of the walls. They were hanging on high scaffolding, risking falling, because the scaffolding had no ladders or horizontal platforms.

It is interesting that in 350 years the Taj Mahal has not sunk a centimeter and has not suffered at all from earthquakes (and in Agra they reach 4 points) and high temperatures (in summer in Agra it is worse than in Delhi, 42-48 degrees). They say that before the construction of the Taj Mahal, the soil conditions, the flow of the Jamuna River, etc. were carefully studied.

Beautiful city of Agra. By the way, the name - Agraban - means paradise in Sanskrit.

Marble factory

At the end we stopped at a marble factory and shop. The marble tables, strewn with precious and semi-precious stones, are incredibly expensive. One of the tables, here in the picture, cost 23 thousand dollars. They showed how marble is chiseled and stones are inserted into it: onyx, agate, sapphire, lapis lazuli, cornelian, jasper. The local marble is very hard, it does not scratch, does not absorb liquid (the shop owner demonstratively spilled Coca-Cola on the marble table) and spreads light . And cornelian itself glows - the entire stone glows if a beam of light is directed at any part of it.

 

Polio vaccination was organised all over India today. In all public places there were two people with vaccines. Loudspeakers were urging people to get vaccinated.

 

Train from Agra to Delhi - Shatabdi Express

This is considered a high-class train, although the carriages were quite shabby. The journey from Agra to Delhi cost only 410 rupees, or 8 dollars. And on top of that, they fed you lunch on the train!!! Hot soup plus a vegetarian or meat dish plus ice cream. The conductor ran around the carriage and found out who would eat what: Veg, non-veg? Veg, non-veg. After lunch, they served grains to nibble on, because Indians cant live without them.

The train was coming from Bhopal and it was 1.5 hours late to Agra. Maybe because of fog. But then the fog thickened and we arrived in Delhi at 2am instead of 10:50pm.

On the way to the hotel, the fog was very thick. The driver turned on the flashing lights, constantly wiped the windows, and in my opinion, he was guided only by the edge of the sidewalk. Nothing was visible ahead. Sometimes he would pull up behind another car, then its lights could still be seen at a distance of 10 meters.
