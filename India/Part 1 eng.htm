Travel notes in India

Madras - Kanchipuram - Mahabalipuram - Bombay - Jaipur - Amber - Fatehpur - Sikandra - Agra Delhi

December 2002 - January 2003

 

Part I. Poverty is not a vice (from the life of Sudra)

 

Behind the airport glass was India. Heat, crowds and crush - that's how I always imagined it. We made our way through the square, packed with colorful and sweaty people, to the taxi stand... And the myth was immediately dispelled. They were just people meeting us, who were not allowed into the Chennai airport building. The taxi easily sped into the city, overtaking bullocks, carts and auto rickshaws, and ahead were two weeks of completely different impressions...

 

 

 

Weekdays

 

It was clear from the first minutes: in Chennai, girls study , boys play cricket, and everyone else works hard. Flocks of girls came out of schools and colleges and lined up at the bus stop, the entire beach of the Bay of Bengal was given over to cricket, and around them, hard everyday life flowed. People laid pavements by hand, in the mud, carried bags on bicycles with the backs of bicycles , and three-wheeled yellow motor scooters-taxi (motor rickshaws) scurried between them . Some carried bags of goods and plates of food on their heads.

 

Then we saw how both in the city and in the villages, manual labor is used everywhere. Well, approximately from the Stone Age. Cobblestone pavement is laid by hand. Hay, goods are transported on camels, especially in Rajasthan. Stonemasons split stone blocks with numerous blows of a hammer on a chisel. And on the construction of houses and roads, women work the same as men. They carry cement, bricks on their heads.

 

The population is so huge that labor costs pennies, and therefore it is easier for business owners to hire an extra hundred people than to use any mechanization. Finished products (such as souvenirs) cost so little that the wages of stonemasons must be negligible. It is unlikely that they had any education other than one craft passed down from their father.

 

Business

 

By the way, about business. It turns out that the basis of their small business is not the quality of the goods, but exclusively in competition. They sell dirty suitcases, but will offer to buy them ten times. The Internet is in a dirty, hot room. Small shops are also dirty and shabby.  

 

But you have to get ahead of the competition. When you leave the hotel, several auto rickshaws will offer you their services, stealing customers from each other. They will simply stand in your way and ask you where you are going , why you are going , let me give you a ride. From the hotel to the bay, probably 25 auto rickshaws stopped and offered (on average, 3 times each) a ride. In general, business is about ripping off someone else or persuading a third, but not about improving your product.

 

At all tourist spots we are attacked by private sellers of souvenirs, postcards, sandals. They don't give us a break. The price is reduced from 10 rupees to 10 cents in half a minute, but the goods must be offered at least 20 times.

 

Streets

 

The cities of Madras, Bombay, and especially Delhi are quite beautifully laid out. Neat squares and thoroughfares with parks and monuments. However, the streets are not at all arranged for pedestrians. Sidewalks, if any, are half a meter wide. And there are a lot of pedestrians. Everyone presses to the edge of the road, jumping away from cars, bicycles, motorcycles and scooters.

 

The main means of transportation is the motor rickshaw , a small, nimble, three-wheeled yellow scooter. It works like a taxi and even has a meter, although it is more efficient to negotiate with the driver. In any case, a motor rickshaw is half the price of a regular car (they would say "air-conditioned taxi").

 

There are no windows or doors on buses and auto rickshaws (and of course, they are full of people, so the answer to the famous riddle is obvious: it's a bus in Madras!). That is, there are openings for windows and doors, but no glass or actual doors. Some people get on and off the bus while it's moving. Since buses are not air-conditioned, and the temperature in Madras rarely drops below 30, the windows would have been opened anyway - so it was wise not to have them at all.

 

Absolutely all drivers are aggressors . They honk, overtake in the oncoming lane, push smaller vehicles to the edge. They cannot share the road with other cars in principle: they drive easily, taking up two lanes, across each other, trying to overtake at all costs. Traffic rules exist as an empty formality. At five in the morning on the way to the airport, the taxi driver pressed the red light just like that. And on the way to Kanchipuram, the driver of our bus often overtook oncoming cars right in front of their noses . He honked loudly, and they let him pass.

 

Sometimes the bus had to push its way through the crowd. That 's why the driver travels with an assistant, who whistles to passersby and motorbikes. Shantanu, a young statistician from a small north Indian university and my companion on excursions around Tamil Nadu, says that the bus from his house to the university literally goes through a bazaar. People lay out their goods on the road, the bus comes, they put the goods away, and then lay them out again after the bus .

 

It is very dirty. Not a single clean corner. Everywhere there is garbage, papers, broken bottles and bricks, food waste. In poor neighborhoods people sweep garbage from their houses directly into the street, not caring much about it afterwards. Passers-by do not make the slightest attempt not to litter, to find a trash can (and there are really very few trash cans). Apparently, this is part of the culture. As a matter of course, quite decently dressed men urinate right on the sidewalks, both in the city and in the suburbs, unless they turn away from the main road!

 

In addition , there are many old, probably sick cows, donkeys, and oxen wandering right along the streets . They say that no one needs these animals. The cows are old, they don't give milk, and it is forbidden to kill a cow in India (poultry, rams, pigs are allowed, but all their beef is imported). So loving owners simply release the cows onto the street, where they wander, eat in garbage heaps, until they die right there on the street. Then some special service removes them.

 

A pair of bullocks (not counting me) in front of a temple in central Madras

 

Some cows may not be thrown out on the street. There is little space in the house , and the owners let them out during the day so that the movement is good for their health. Then they seem to come home on their own.

 

The mass poverty is shocking. Many people are very poorly dressed. Many, in fact, have no clothes at all, they are simply wrapped in rotten rags. Wells on the embankment, and people get water in buckets, wash linen, carpets, dishes and children right there ... Homeless people have settled under the bridges... Naked children right in the city, cattle roaming nearby without much supervision , homeless dogs. Many walk barefoot. Children run after passers-by, especially when they spot tourists, and beg for alms.

 

However, that's nothing... The poor quarters of Bombay on the shore of the Arabian Sea are difficult to describe. These are completely dilapidated, but inhabited huts, just leaky roofs on a few sticks sticking out. It looks like a gypsy camp. Enormous arrays of dilapidated huts stretch for kilometers and are clearly visible when flying in. The huts come right up to the airport fence , right at the beginning of the runway. They are practically adjacent to each other. The passages between the huts are almost invisible.

 

Gentlemen

 

A few years ago, the Indians had an epiphany and, in an effort to shed their colonial past, they renamed their major cities. So Madras became Chennai, Bombay became Mumbai and Calcutta became Calcutta. They are now considered to sound less British and more local.

 

And yet they have been a colony for too long and too recently. It is astonishing for the 21st century, but it is felt everywhere. They believe without a shadow of a doubt that everyone would be delighted to feel like a slave owner or at least a master. In a hotel you constantly hear  Yes, sir, You are our guest, sir, and we are here to serve you, etc. Bent over double, with a cloying smile, they open the doors, street and elevator. They grab bags and drag them away, even very small packages . In a public toilet, one attendant opened and closed the water tap for me, pressed the soap dish and handed me napkins!

 

In fact, for all their helpfulness, they are not very useful. Getting a competent answer to a simple question is not trivial.

 

As in other countries, one's position in society is emphasized by one's appearance. It is quite normal for both women and men to paint their faces and hands. At the opening of the conference, one elderly professor had three multi -colored bright stripes across his forehead. Women are obliged to wrap themselves in their national clothes. The richer the woman, the brighter her rags.

 

It is interesting that their skin color also differs and clearly correlates with their social status. The intelligentsia, students, guides, stewardesses, and employees are lighter, the drivers are darker, and the road builders and peasants are very dark, much blacker than American blacks .

 

Reference books proudly claim that modern India is the world's largest democracy. At the same time, their social inequality is extreme. It is formally expressed in their caste system.

 

About castes and marriages

 

On the way from Jaipur to Agra, the driver Sunil gave an exclusive interview to the popular newspaper Shesterenka . On the topic of castes and marriages. According to him, 80% of all marriages are arranged by parents, 100% of them are successful. The remaining 20% ​​of marriages are for love, but only half of them are successful. The parents agree, find out through numerous relatives and agents all the characteristics of the potential bride, pay an official visit, and if everything is ok, then a ceremonial viewing is organized at the bride's house, where at a common table, in the obligatory presence of all the parents, the young people are allowed to see each other and exchange a few words. If everything is ok then, the young people can meet several more times, but as a rule, the decision to marry is made soon. It cannot be otherwise, since the parents, thank God, have already found out everything and approved. However, Sunil himself has already rejected several brides - at their very first meeting.

 

Before the wedding, the bride is not allowed to visit the groom's house under any circumstances, even in the company of her parents. After the wedding, the bride moves to the groom's parents' house . Sometimes the opposite happens, but there must be good reasons for this :

 

       when the bride's parents are quite rich, and the groom's parents, on the contrary, are poor (but this is rare, as a rule, the couple is selected based on equal caste and level),

 

       when the bride's parents have her as their only daughter, and they promise to treat their son-in-law as a son,

 

       when the bride's parents promise the groom a car, a job, money, etc. if they move in with them.

 

Sunil and I on the banks of the holy river Yamuna near the Taj Mahal in Agra

 

An interesting solution to the housing problem. Sunil's parents have their own house with a flat roof. When Sunil gets married, he will not leave his parents' house, but will hire builders and build a couple of rooms right on the roof, i.e. an extra floor.

 

In general, Indians try not to rent an apartment, but to buy at least a small piece of land and build a house there. This gives two important advantages: the house can be rebuilt, and also, on the ground floor, you can set up a shop. Many do so, therefore on all streets, in most houses, tiny businesses are set up: rags, juices, cereals, souvenirs, bicycle repair, etc.

 

Sunil himself is from the 3rd caste, "Gujati" or "Gupta", i.e. businessmen and traders. So his parents will find him a bride only among the traders. Above him are 2 castes: Brahmins (intellectuals and priests) and Rajputs ( warriors). All that is under his caste is called "Sudra" (literally - "untouchable"), the poor, and they make up the overwhelming majority in India.

 

Temples

 

South India is rich in colorful temples. Once a motor rickshaw took me around Madras. He showed me three main temples: one to the god Krishna, another to the god Shiva, and the third Christian (San Thom Cathedral), built on the bones of the Apostle Thomas. The Apostle was killed right here in Madras. That's why his bone is on display on the wall of the temple for anyone who wants to see it.

 

St. Tome Cathedral in Madras

 

Then there were excursions to Mahabalipuram and Kancheepuram.

 

Kanch i Puram translates as "golden city". It is the temple capital of India. The highest concentration of Hindu temples; in one city there are 125: one hundred to Krishna, eighteen to Shiva, the rest are Buddhist . No temples are built to the god Brahma.

 

Beautiful multi-storeyed shelving units  gopuram  are built at the gates to the temples . The number of storeys in a gopuram is always ODD. Explanation: if divided by 2, the remainder is 1, i.e., everyone has one god. (And Krishna, Shiva, Bramha his 3 types). Some temples date back to the 6th-8th centuries.

 

The majestic gopuram above the entrance to the Hindu temple in Kanchipuram

 

In temples:

 

       numerous images of Krishna and Shiva [ Dancing Shiva . Shiva took part in a dance competition. During the dance, he dropped his hat, but picked it up with his left foot and put it on. The judges decided that Shiva had invented a new dance position and awarded him the victory!] Gods are not very different from people in their description and behavior . They are born and die, are reborn, get married, have children.

 

       numerous chariots and images of different animals. The chariot festival is one of the main ones, when they go in processions. (Recalls "Hare Krishna, Hare Rama" along Nevsky Prospect.)

 

       a square pond in which people actually wash themselves, with soap and a washcloth.

 

       Mango tree. The mango trees growing on the temple grounds have miraculous properties in terms of wish fulfillment. After eating its fruits, girls get married and have children. Having children, users of mango fruits send large donations to the temple.

 

       oil candles. In general, light penetrates into the depths of the temple through windows under the roof. The design and location of the temple are thought out so that the most important and sacred corners are illuminated during sunrise .

 

       painted floors. Chalk drawings, and usually imprints from standard stamps. Therefore, they are regularly updated. Stamps are sold nearby.

 

       of course, cripples and beggars.

 

     

This ancient temple is built right into the sand

 

On the way we stopped at the site of Rajiv Gandhi's assassination , just between Madras airport and Kanchipuram. He was campaigning for a second term, flew into Madras and gave a speech on the way. When he finished, a woman from Sri Lanka came up and bent down to kiss his shoes. A man came up from the other side and also bent down. The woman turned out to be a human bomb, a suicide bomber.

 

We also visited the silk factory in Kanch i Puram . Fabrics with complex designs are made by hand. The design is programmed on large hard PUNCH CARDS. A chain of punch cards is fed into the loom and controls which thread goes up or down at what point .

 

They need to weave three pieces with the same pattern to cover their expenses. And if they mechanize it, they need 20 pieces. They can't sell that, so it's more profitable to use manual labor. In the silk shop, I was served by a girl of about 12 years old - very professional, detailed explanations!

 

We passed a beautiful amusement park (TsPKO!). Alas, it is not for people, but for filming movies.

 

Everywhere we are greeted as beloved gentlemen. Lunches are prepared in advance: one - on the very shore of the Bay of Bengal, practically on the beach, the other - on the hotel veranda.

 

At Shantanu's insistence, I tried his favorite dish: pineapple with hot chili powder. So- so. Large coconuts are sold cheaply on the street. The vendor cuts off the top of the coconut with one blow, inserts a straw, and you can drink a whole liter of cool coconut water.
